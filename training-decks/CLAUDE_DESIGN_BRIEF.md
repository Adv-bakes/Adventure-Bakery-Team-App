# Claude Design Brief — SQF Training Module 1 (Pilot for a 12-module program)

Paste or upload this whole file into a Claude Design session. The goal is a **visually striking,
modern 16:9 training deck** — strong layouts, illustration, depth, energy — that still respects the
hard constraints below. This is the pilot for a 12-module program, so a look that can become a
reusable design system is a plus.

## Who this is for
Production-floor employees of **Adventure Bakery**, a B2B **contract-manufacturing industrial
bakery** preparing for SQF (Safe Quality Food) certification. Tone: warm, confident,
people-first — but the setting is a food **plant**, not a shop.

## Hard constraints (do not break these)
1. **Keep the slide list below as the structure — these slides, in this order.** The count fits
   this module's material (it varies module to module; it is *not* a fixed "always 12"). Our import
   tooling maps narration to slides by position, so once we've agreed on this structure, don't add,
   remove, or reorder slides. If the material is better taught with a different breakdown, say so —
   we'll update the source content to match before importing.
2. Slides are converted to **960×540 PNG images** for an in-app viewer. Everything must stay
   legible at that size: body text ≥ ~16 pt equivalent, high contrast, no fine hairline detail
   that disappears at 96 DPI.
3. **Fonts:** stick to widely available fonts (the conversion server substitutes exotic ones).
4. **Never name customers, retailers, or branded products.** Use exactly: "a major grocery
   retailer" / "our retail customer", "our rum cakes", "our plant-based burgers". Do not write
   any real retailer or brand names on slides.
5. **Industrial imagery only:** production lines/conveyors, mixers/ovens, workers in
   **bouffant caps/hairnets and aprons**, QA clipboards, shipping boxes/pallets/trucks.
   **Never** storefronts, cafés, chef hats, or retail-shop scenes.
6. Don't write speaker notes — our tooling injects the approved narration afterwards.
7. Export the final result as **PPTX**.

## Brand
- Palette: dark warm brown `#3B2F2F`, amber `#C8873C`, cream `#FFF8F0`, safe-green `#4A7C59`
  (feel free to add tints/shades of these for depth — gradients, washes, duotones welcome).
- Brand name on title + footers: **Adventure Bakery** · program name: **SQF Food Safety Training**.

## The 12 slides (title · on-slide text · what the visual should communicate)

1. **Module 1 — Why We're Doing This** · subtitle "SQF Food Safety Training" · Hero title slide;
   industrial bakery energy (production line / conveyor motif).
2. **Learning Objectives** · "By the end of this module you will be able to:" with four points:
   ① Explain SQF and why we're pursuing it ② Connect it to a major retail opportunity
   ③ Describe food safety culture and your role ④ Know your authority to stop unsafe product ·
   Four distinct objective cards/blocks with iconography.
3. **What is SQF?** · "An internationally recognized food safety certification." S = Safe,
   Q = Quality, F = Food. Caption: "An independent auditor verifies we make safe food the same
   way, every day — and that we can prove it." · Acronym treatment + certification/credential motif.
4. **Why certification opens doors (GFSI)** · "SQF is benchmarked by GFSI — the bar major
   retailers require." Diagram: GFSI as the umbrella standard over SQF (ours — highlight it),
   BRCGS, FSSC 22000. Caption: "One certification tells every big customer our food is safe —
   without each auditing us separately."
5. **The retail opportunity** · "No certification, no shelf space." Progression: SQF
   certification → GFSI recognition → shelf space with a major grocery retailer · B2B
   shipping/distribution feel (truck, pallets — not a shop).
6. **Our customer may visit us directly** · "Our retail customer reserves the right to audit our
   facility — sometimes unannounced." Mon–Fri all checked: "Ready every day — not just audit
   day." · Inspection/audit-readiness motif.
7. **What "food safety culture" means** · "Doing the right thing even when no one is watching."
   Message chip: "Everyone owns food safety" · A team of plant workers (bouffant caps/hairnets,
   aprons) — diverse, positive.
8. **Your role on a rotating team** · "Every station is your station — the same standard
   everywhere." Cycle: Prep → Mixing → Baking → Packaging, center: "One team, one standard".
9. **You can stop the line** · "See contamination, a wrong label, or a possible allergen mix-up?
   You have the authority and responsibility to report it and stop product from moving.
   **You will never be in trouble for raising a concern.**" · The emotional peak of the module —
   make it land (STOP visual, raised hand, etc.). Use the safe-green for the reassurance line.
10. **A preview of the allergen-free promise** · "The biggest challenge we own together — more on
    this in later modules." Two-sided comparison: LEFT (green) "Our plant-based burgers — made
    free of the top 9 allergens, a promise our customers count on" vs RIGHT (amber) "The same
    building also handles wheat • egg • milk, with shared equipment and rotating staff."
    Footer chip: "Our shared job: keep them apart."
11. **What an auditor might ask you** · "Be ready to answer in your own words:" three quoted
    questions: "Why is the company getting SQF certified?" / "What would you do if you saw
    something unsafe?" / "Whose job is food safety here?" · Auditor-with-clipboard motif.
12. **Summary** · Checklist: ✓ SQF proves we make safe food ✓ It's our door to a major retail
    customer ✓ Culture means everyone owns food safety ✓ You can always report and stop unsafe
    product · Closing chip: "Next: Personal Hygiene & GMPs".
